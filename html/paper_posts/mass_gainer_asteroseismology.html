    A Case Study of a Binary Mass Gainer in the SPB Instability Strip

    A star that undergoes accretion from a binary companion retains a significantly different internal structure compared to a single star of the same mass. This has big impacts on its asteroseismic signals and implications for how we fit models to stars that have accreted mass.

    Setting the Scene

    Our goal with this paper was to better understand how the changes in structure that result from mass transfer impact a stars asteroseismic signals. Asteroseismology is incredibly useful as it allows to learn about a stars internal structure by measuring the ways in which it pulsates.

    In particular, we target Slowly Pulsating B stars (SPBs), which given their mass range and expected to be both numerous and also frequently in binary systems. We think that around a quarter of these systems will be part of an interacting binary system (which means they'll experience mass transfer).

    So the aim of the paper was to simulate one of these systems, track how its internal structure changes and how its asteroseismic signals change as a result. Let's get into it!

    Simulating a binary

    The first thing we need is a fully evolved binary system. Luckily, doing these sorts of simulations in 1D is made convenient with the open-source code MESA!

    A word on MESA

    We used the MESA 1D stellar evolution code to simulate a binary system. MESA is a powerful tool that allows us to simulate the evolution of stars and binary systems in great detail. You can read the paper for the exact details on the exact settings that we used, but one thing to keep in mind is that we don't include rotation in any of our simulations.

    If you get interested in running models like ours or some of your own, you can check out the GitHub repo for this project and the Zenodo archive to find scripts to run models and the data for the core models in the paper.

    Stellar evolution

    Now let's take a look at how this system evolves. In the figure on the right you can see the Hertzsprung-Russel diagram for the evolution of this binary. This is the classic evolution diagram for stellar evolution and describes how a star's luminosity and temperature changes over time.

    On this plot you can see that there are two tracks, one for the donor star (the one that transfers mass onto its companion) and one for the accretor star (which receives material from its companion).

    You can check out the paper for a detailed description of what exactly is happening in this plot. But what I'd like you to take away is that, at first glance, it seems that the accretor star seems to evolve just like a more massive single star after the accretion. Look at how the track (underlined in green) after point A4 almost exactly lines up with the dashed line of a 3.5 solar mass single star. So it seems like accretion doesn't affect how the star evolves?? Did I lie to you before?!

    The key question to ask is: what's going on inside the accretor star?

    Internal structure evolution

    Now let's take a look at how the abundance of hydrogen inside the star changes over time. We're going to compare a single star (in orange) to our accretor model (in purple).

    Each panel in this plot shows a different timestep (which is annotated based on how much hydrogen is left at the star's centre, $X_c$). The $x$-axis is a function of mass coordinate inside the star (just think of it as the left being the centre and the right being further out), the axis is also zoomed in mostly on the centre of the star.

    The takeaway point from this plot is that the hydrogen mass fraction profile for the mass-gainer has a kink in the distribution around $M \approx 0.8 {\rm M_{\odot}}$ as a result of accretion. And critically, this kink doesn't go away over time.

    This shows that the internal structure of an accretor remains significantly different from an equivalent single star throughout its main sequence.

    A story about deriving the Brunt-Väisälä Frequency

    Perhaps my favourite part of this project was reading the original papers for the derivation of the Brunt-Väisälä Frequency. This frequency describes the regions of convective instability and can be useful for determining the period distribution of g-modes in a star. I have plots showing the distribution of this frequency within the star over time in the paper.

    But that's not what this aside is about, this about the original derivation of the Brunt-Väisälä frequency.

    It turns out that this frequency is actually very important in atmospheric sciences and cloud formation and so was actually first derived independently by two different meteorologists.

    In 1925, Finnish meteorologist Vilho Väisälä first published a derivation of this frequency in a Finnish journal written in German. Later in 1927, David Brunt independently derived the frequency, publishing it in English in the Quarterly Journal of the Royal Meteorological Society.

    In an update to the paper, Brunt acknowledged the paper by Väisälä but claimed that he'd derived in lectures in 1921-24 previously and published his work anyway. To this day his name is still used first in reference to the frequency despite Väisälä first publishing it.

    On a more amusing note, the paper directly following Brunt's in the journal concerns The Soaring Flight of Birds, in which "the bird was kept under constant observation through a range-finder for no less than 45 minutes". But the reason I bring this up is because they include several figures of clouds that they observed whilst watching for the bird. And these figures do not detail cloud formation or evolution, but are simply included because the authors thought they looked like certain animals!!

    In summary, publishing was slightly different in the 1920s.
